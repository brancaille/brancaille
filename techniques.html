Techniques and Brutality

  • Strikes: fists, feet, elbows, knees, head
  • Throws and ground submissions
  • "Southpaw guard" to destabilize opponents
  • No protective gear: endurance and flexibility required

Festive and Deadly Duality

  • Lucho d’ome : victory by pinning the opponent on their back and holding for a few seconds, knee on chest. Holds allowed from head to belt + leg sweeps; grabbing legs with hands forbidden.
  • Lucho de la centuro en aut (aka “lutte à mains plates” – palms pressed at start) : no leg holds. Throws only with upper body. This Provençal style later became French, then Roman, and finally Greco-Roman wrestling.
  • Lucho a bono man : like the previous but allowed holds below the belt and leg hooks. Close to Lucha d’Home except head holds were forbidden. Similar to Breton Gouren, except wrestlers fought bare-chested.
  • Lucho des miche ome (for adolescents) : strikes and throws, no ground fighting.
  • Lucho Libro : everything allowed except strikes; fight continues on the ground until submission. In some regions strikes were permitted and it was indistinguishable from Brancaille.
  • Adult Brancaille : everything allowed until submission. Referees intervene only in case of extreme danger.
